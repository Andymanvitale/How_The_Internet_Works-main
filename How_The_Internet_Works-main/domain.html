Domain Names and ICANN

Domain names are the addresses of the internet. If you want to create a website about your favorite knitting supplies, then how will anyone find your website? Surely they won't be able to find your website by it's IP address, so they will need to use a domain name. A domain name is what appears at the top of your browser whenever you go to a website. It's created by the person that is either hosting or built the website, and usually made for someone to remember. Each domain name is connected to an IP address so your computer will know what to request and what to expect when you type in a website in your search bar.

So, who regulates these names?

The Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers, or more easily remembered as ICANN, regulates and ensures that there is digital space and a continued support for domain names and creates any regulation they see fit for the continued use of the internet. They created a structure of the naming of domain names, and using an image from Wikipedia, it will be broken down into a simple guide. It's not difficult, and using this format for all of the internet is key for it's continued growth.

ICANN is also responsible for the implementation and development of both IPv4 and IPv6. They also focus on the global Domain Naming System, or DNS. DNS was developed and adopted for an international standard of naming. This standard is still followed today and can be broken down into multiple parts. Each part has a meaning and relates to one another in a specific way.

Looking at the diagram, the link en.wikipedia.org is broken down into a few categories. Starting at the top, the period is used to separate any labels which would follow. Next is the .org which is the top level domain, or TLD. After that is the second level domain and finally the en. is the subdomain. Each website follows this general breakdown, usually websites start with www.

When you type in a website, you'll probably follow this style. However, web browsers have gotten better recently at determining where you will end up and the subdomain can be excluded in certain instances. Going to google.com and www.google.com will lead to the same place.
